Exam 2025#

1.   Let \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\) be matrices defined as follows:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} A &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, & B &= \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 8 & 9 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, & C &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*}\end{split}\]

(a)   Calculate \(AB\).

(b)   Determine whether the matrix multiplication of \(A\) and \(B\) is commutative.

(c)   Calculate the determinant of \(C\).

(d)   For matrix \(A\), verify that \(\det(A) = \det(A^\mathsf{T})\).

(e)   Calculate the inverse of \(C\).

Solution

(a)  

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} AB &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 8 & 9 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 & 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 + 4 + 9 & 8 + 2 + 18 & 9 + 8 + 15 \\ 0 + 8 + 15 & 0 + 4 + 30 & 0 + 16 + 25 \\ 7 + 0 + 18 & 8 + 0 + 36 & 9 + 0 + 30 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 20 & 28 & 32 \\ 23 & 34 & 41 \\ 25 & 44 & 39 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(b)   Calculate \(BA\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} BA &= \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 8 & 9 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 16 & \cdot & \cdot \\ \cdot & \cdot & \cdot \\ \cdot & \cdot & \cdot \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So \(AB \neq BA\) and \(A\) and \(B\) do not commute.

(c)  

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \det(C) &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = 2 \begin{vmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 \end{vmatrix} - \begin{vmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 \end{vmatrix} = 2(4) + 4 = 12. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(d)  

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \det(A) &= \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 6 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 0 & 6 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{vmatrix} = 24 - 2 = 22, \\ \det(A^\mathsf{T}) &= \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 & 0 \\ 3 & 5 & 6 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 5 & 6 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 5 \end{vmatrix} = 24 -2 = 22 = \det(A) \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(e)  

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \det(C) &= 12,\\ \adj(C) &= \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 4 & -12 \\ -2 & 4 & 0 \\ 1 & -2 & 6 \end{pmatrix}^\mathsf{T} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 & 1 \\ 4 & 4 & -2 \\ -12 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, \\ C^{-1} &= \frac{\adj(C)}{\det(C)} = \frac{1}{12} \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 & 1 \\ 4 & 4 & -2 \\ -12 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{1}{6} & \frac{1}{12} \\ \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{3} & -\frac{1}{6} \\ -1 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

2.   Consider the following system of linear equations:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} 2 x_1 - 3 x_2 + x_3 &= 7, \\ 4 x_1 + x_2 - 2 x_3 &= -1, \\ -x_1 + 2 x_2 + 3 x_3 &= 3. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(a)   Solve the system using Cramer’s rule.

(b)   Solve the system using Gaussian elimination.

(c)   Calculate the inverse of the coefficient matrix using Gauss-Jordan elimination.

Solution

(a)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} x_1 &= \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} 7 & -3 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 & -2 \\ 3 & 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} }{ \begin{vmatrix} 2 & -3 & 1 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} } = \frac{ 7 \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + 3 \begin{vmatrix} -1 & -2 \\ 3 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 3 & 2 \end{vmatrix} }{ 2 \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + 3 \begin{vmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix} } = \frac{49 + 9 - 5}{14 + 30 + 9} = \frac{53}{53} = 1, \\ x_2 &= \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 7 & 1 \\ 4 & -1 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 & 3 \end{vmatrix} }{53} = \frac{ 2 \begin{vmatrix} -1 & - 2 \\ 3 & 3 \end{vmatrix} - 7 \begin{vmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} 4 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} }{53} = \frac{6 - 70 + 11}{53} = \frac{-53}{53} = -1, \\ x_3 &= \frac{ \begin{vmatrix} 2 & -3 & 7 \\ 4 & 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} }{53} = \frac{ 2 \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + 3 \begin{vmatrix} 4 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + 7 \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix} }{53} = \frac{10 + 33 + 63}{53} = \frac{106}{53} = 2. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(b)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -3 & 1 & 7 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 & 3 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} -1 & 2 & 3 & 3 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 & -1 \\ 2 & -3 & 1 & 7 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} -R_1 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -2 & -3 & -3 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 & -1 \\ 2 & -3 & 1 & 7 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 - 4R_1 \\ R_3 - 2R_1 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -2 & -3 & -3 \\ 0 & 9 & 10 & 11 \\ 0 & 1 & 7 & 13 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -2 & -3 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & 7 & 13 \\ 0 & 9 & 10 & 11 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \\ R_3 - 9R_2 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -2 & -3 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & 7 & 13 \\ 0 & 0 & -53 & -106 \end{array} \right). \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} x_3 &= \frac{1}{-53}(-106) = 2, \\ x_2 &= \frac{1}{1}(13 - 7(2)) = -1, \\ x_1 &= \frac{1}{1}(-3 + 2(-1) + 3(2)) = 1. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(c)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & -3 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \frac{1}{2}R_1 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & -\frac{3}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 4 & 1 & -2 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 3 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 - 4R_1 \\ R_3 + R_1 \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & -\frac{3}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 & -4 & -2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{7}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \frac{1}{7}R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & -\frac{3}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{4}{7} & -\frac{2}{7} & \frac{1}{7} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{7}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 + \frac{3}{2} R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \\ R_3 - \frac{1}{2}R_2 \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 0 & -\frac{5}{14} & \frac{1}{14} & \frac{3}{14} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{4}{7} & -\frac{2}{7} & \frac{1}{7} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5\frac{3}{14} & \frac{9}{14} & -\frac{1}{14} & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \\ \frac{14}{53}R_3 \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 0 & -\frac{5}{14} & \frac{1}{14} & \frac{3}{14} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{4}{7} & -\frac{2}{7} & \frac{1}{7} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \frac{9}{53} & -\frac{1}{53} & \frac{14}{53} \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 + \frac{5}{14}R_3 \\ R_2 + \frac{4}{7}R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & \frac{7}{53} & 1\frac{1}{53} & \frac{5}{53} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -\frac{10}{53} & \frac{7}{53} & \frac{8}{53} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \frac{9}{53} & -\frac{1}{53} & \frac{14}{53} \end{array} \right). \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So \(A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{7}{53} & 1\frac{1}{53} & \frac{5}{53} \\ -\frac{10}{53} & \frac{7}{53} & \frac{8}{53} \\ \frac{9}{53} & -\frac{1}{53} & \frac{14}{53} \end{pmatrix}\).

3.   Let \(\mathbf{u}\), \(\mathbf{v}\), \(\mathbf{w}\) and \(\mathbf{x}\) be vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) such that

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \vec{u} &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}, & \vec{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}, & \vec{w} &= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, & \vec{x} &= \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

where \(a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}\)

(a)   Compute \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}\).

(b)   Compute \(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}\).

(c)   What is the angle between \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\)?

(d)   For what values of \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) is \(\mathbf{x}\) orthogonal to both \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\)?

(e)   Express the vector \(\mathbf{y} = (1, -7, 15)\) as a linear combination of \(\mathbf{u}\), \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\).

Solution

(a)   \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 2(1) + (-1)(4) + 3(-2) = -8\)

(b)   \(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 & -2 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -10 \\ 7 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}\)

(c)   \(\theta = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w}}{\| \mathbf{v} \| \| \mathbf{w} \|} \right) = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{-3}{\sqrt{21}\sqrt{2}} \right) = 2.052.\)

(d)   Since \(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{w}\) is orthogonal to both \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) then \(\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{w}\).

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{w} &= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 2 & -1 & 3 \\ -1 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

So \(a = k\), \(b = 5k\) and \(c = k\) where \(k \in \mathbb{R}\).

(e)   Need to find the solution of

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 4 & 0 \\ 3 & -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \\ \alpha_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -7 \\ 15 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Using Gauss-Jordan elimination:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 4 & 0 & -7 \\ 3 & -2 & 1 & 15 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 \leftrightarrow R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} -1 & 4 & 0 & -7 \\ 2 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 & 1 & 15 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} -R_1 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -4 & 0 & 7 \\ 2 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 & 1 & 15 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 - 2R_1 \\ R_3 - 3R_1 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -4 & 0 & 7 \\ 0 & 9 & -1 & -13 \\ 0 & 10 & 1 & -6 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \frac{1}{9}R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -4 & 0 & 7 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{9} & -\frac{13}{9} \\ 0 & 10 & 1 & -6 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 + 4R_2 \\ \\ R_3 - 10R_2 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -\frac{4}{9} & 1\frac{1}{9} \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{9} & -\frac{13}{9} \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{19}{9} & \frac{76}{9} \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \\ \frac{9}{19}R_3 \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -\frac{4}{9} & 1\frac{1}{9} \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{9} & -\frac{13}{9} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 + \frac{4}{9}R_3 \\ R_2 + \frac{1}{9}R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 \end{array} \right) \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So \(3 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} + 4 \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -7 \\ 15 \end{pmatrix}\).

4.   Consider the points \(A = (1,2,3)\), \(B = (4,-1,2)\) and \(C = (2, 3, -4)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

(a)   Determine the vector equation of the line passing through the points \(A\) and \(B\) in the standard basis.

(b)   Find an equation of the plane that passes through the points \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\).

(c)   What is the shortest distance between the point with co-ordinates \((10, -5, 3)\) and the plane from part (b)?

(d)   Find the equation of the line where the plane described by the equation \(x + 2y - 3z = 4\) intersects with the plane from part (b).

Solution

(a)   Let \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) then the vector equation of the line passing through \(A\) and \(B\) is

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{a} + t (\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} + t \left( \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \right) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} + t \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + 3t \\ 2 - 3t \\ 3 - t \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(b)   The point-normal equation for a plane is \(\mathbf{n} \cdot \left( \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} - \mathbf{a} \right) = \mathbf{0}\).

Calculate the normal to the plane:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{n} &= \left( \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \right) \times \left( \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \right) \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 3 & -3 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 & -7 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 22 \\ 20 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} = 3 \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 10 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Calculate the point-normal form:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{p}) &= 0 \\ \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 10 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x - 1 \\ y - 2 \\ z - 3 \end{pmatrix} &= 0 \\ 11x + 10y + 3z &= 40. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(c)   The shortest distance, \(d\), of a point with position vector \(\mathbf{q}\) and a plane passing through a point with position vector \(\mathbf{p}\) and has a normal vector \(\mathbf{n}\) is \( d = (\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p})\cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\).

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} d &= ( \mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}) \cdot \frac{\mathbf{n}}{\| \mathbf{n} \|} \\ &= \left( \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ -5 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \right) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{230}} \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 10 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 9 \\ -7 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} \frac{11}{\sqrt{230}} \\ \frac{10}{\sqrt{230}} \\ \frac{3}{\sqrt{230}} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \frac{29\sqrt{230}}{230} \approx 1.9122. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(d)   The direction vector for the line of intersection is perpendicular to both planes so

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{d} &= \begin{pmatrix} 11 \\ 10 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 11 & 10 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & -3 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -36 \\ 36 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix} = 3 \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

We also need a point which both planes pass through

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} 11x + 10y + 3z &= 40,\\ x + 2y - 3z &= 4, \end{align*} \end{split}\]

summing both equations gives

\[ \begin{align*} 12x + 12y &= 44. \end{align*} \]

let \(x = 0\) then \(y = \dfrac{11}{3}\) and \(z = \dfrac{10}{9}\) so both planes pass through the point with co-ordinates \(\left(0, \dfrac{11}{3}, \dfrac{10}{9}\right)\).

So the equation of the line is

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ \frac{11}{3} \\ \frac{10}{9} \end{pmatrix} + t \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -3t \\ -\frac{11}{3} + 3t \\ \frac{10}{9} + t \end{pmatrix} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} -27t \\ 33 + 27t \\ 10 + 9t \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

5.   Consider the vector space \(V\) over the field \(\mathbb{R}\) with basis vectors

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{v}_1 &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}, & \mathbf{v}_2 &= \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(a)   Determine whether the set \(T = \{ \mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}_1 = 0 \}\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

(b)   Show that the vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\) are linearly independent.

(c)   Extend the set \(\{ \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \}\) to a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Justify your choice of the additional vector.

(d)   Find the change of basis matrix that changes from the standard basis to your basis from part (c).

(e)   Using your change of basis matrix from part (c) or otherwise, express the vector \(\mathbf{u} = (1, 1, 4)\) with respect to your basis from part (c).

Solution

(a)   Determine whether the set \(T = \{ \mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}_1 = 0 \}\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Let \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in T\) such that \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\). Using the subspace condition

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \mathbf{u} + \alpha \mathbf{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{pmatrix} + \alpha \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + \alpha v_1 \\ u_2 + \alpha v_2 \\ u_3 + \alpha v_3 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Checking that \(\mathbf{u} + \alpha \mathbf{v} \in T\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + \alpha v_1 \\ u_2 + \alpha v_2 \\ u_3 + \alpha v_3 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} &= 0 \\ u_1 + 2u_3 + \alpha v_1 + 2\alpha v_3 &= 0, \end{align*} \end{split}\]

since by the definition of \(T\), \(u_1 + 2u_3 = 0\) and \(v_1 + 2v_3 = 0\) then \(\mathbf{u} + \alpha \mathbf{v} \in T\) so \(T\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

(b)  

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \\ R_3 - 2R_1 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \\ R_3 + R_2 \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{array} \right) \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\) are linearly independent.

(c)   A basis for \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is \(W = \{ \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 \}\) where \(\mathbf{e}_1 = (1, 0, 0)^\mathsf{T}\).

Need to show that \(\mathbf{e}_1\) is linearly independent to \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\). Row reducing \(( \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2 )\) to reduced row echelon form:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -1 \\ \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \frac{1}{2} R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 - R_2 \\ \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} & \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 - \frac{1}{2} R_3 \\ R_2 + \frac{1}{2}R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right) \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So \(W\) is a linearly independent set of vectors which span \(\mathbb{R}^3\) so \(W\) is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

(d)   Using Gauss-Jordan elimination to calculate the inverse of the basis

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ R_2 \leftrightarrow R_1 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} \\ \frac{1}{2}R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 - R_2 \\ \phantom{x} \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0 & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right) \begin{matrix} R_1 - \frac{1}{2}R_3 \\ R_2 + \frac{1}{2}R_3 \\ \phantom{x} \end{matrix} \\ \longrightarrow & \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right) \end{align*} \end{split}\]

So the change of basis matrix is \(A_{E \to W} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).

(e)   Using your change of basis matrix from part (c) or otherwise, express the vector \(\mathbf{u} = (1, 1, 4)\) with respect to your basis from part (c).

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \left[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \right]_W &= \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{3}{2} \\ \frac{5}{2} \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \end{split}\]

6.   Let \(T : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a linear transformation defined by

\[T (x, y, z) = (2x + y - z , 3x - 4y + 2z , -x + 5y + 3z ).\]

(a)   Find the matrix \(A\) that represents the linear transformation such that \(T(\mathbf{u}) = A \mathbf{u}\).

(b)   Use your matrix from part (a) to determine \(T (1, 2, 3)\).

(c)   Does there exist an inverse transformation \(T^{-1}\) such that \(T^{-1}(T(\mathbf{u})) = \mathbf{u}\)? Justify your answer. If an inverse does exist, find the transformation matrix for the inverse transformation.

(d)   A point in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with co-ordinates \((1,2)\) is rotated anti-clockwise about the origin by angle \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) and then translated by the vector \(\mathbf{t} = (5, 2)\). Determine the single matrix that achieves the composite of the two transformations. Use your matrix to calculate the co-ordinates of the transformed point.

Solution

(a)   \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 3 & -4 & 2 \\ -1 & 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\)

(b)   \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 3 & -4 & 2 \\ -1 & 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 18 \end{pmatrix}\)

(c)   An inverse transformation exists if the transformation matrix is non-singular, i.e., \(\det(A) \neq 0\).

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \det(A) = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 3 & -4 & 2 \\ -1 & 5 & 3 \end{vmatrix} = 2 \begin{vmatrix} -4 & 2 \\ 5 & 3 \end{vmatrix} - \begin{vmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} - \begin{vmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ -1 & 5 \end{vmatrix} = -44 - 11 - 11 = -66, \end{align*} \end{split}\]

so an inverse transformation exists.

The transformation matrix is the inverse of \(A\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} A^{-1} &= - \frac{1}{66} \begin{pmatrix} -22 & -11 & 11 \\ -8 & 5 & -11 \\ -2 & -7 & -11 \end{pmatrix}^\mathsf{T} = - \frac{1}{66} \begin{pmatrix} -22 & -8 & -2 \\ -11 & 5 & -7 \\ 11 & -11 & -11 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{3} & \frac{4}{33} & \frac{1}{33} \\ \frac{1}{6} & -\frac{5}{66} & \frac{7}{66} \\ -\frac{1}{6} & \frac{1}{6} & \frac{1}{6} \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

(d)   The composite transformation matrix is \(A = T\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \cdot Rot \left( \dfrac{\pi}{3} \right)\).

Rotation matrix:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} Rot\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) &= \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\frac{\pi}{3}) & \sin(\frac{\pi}{3}) & 0 \\ -\sin(\frac{\pi}{3}) & \cos(\frac{\pi}{3}) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & 0 \\ -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.5 & 0.866 & 0 \\ -0.866 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Translation matrix:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} T \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Composite transformation matrix:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & 0 \\ -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & 5 \\ -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Applying composite transformation to homogeneous co-ordinates

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & 5 \\ -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1\frac{1}{2} + \sqrt{3} \\ 3 - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix} 9.2321 \\ -1.3301 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \end{align*} \end{split}\]

so the transformed point has co-ordinates \((9.2331, -1.3301)\).
